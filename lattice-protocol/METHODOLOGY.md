# Adversarial Multi-AI Consensus Building

## A Methodology for Structural Negotiation Design

### The Problem

Traditional diplomatic and policy frameworks are designed by institutions that share fundamental assumptions. A Brookings paper on US-Iran relations is written by people who think like Americans. A CASS paper on Chinese debt strategy is written by people who think like Chinese policymakers. Neither side stress-tests their framework against the other's actual objections — they stress-test against their model of the other's objections, which is always softer than reality.

This produces frameworks that look elegant on paper and collapse on contact with adversarial counterparties.

### The Method

Adversarial Multi-AI Consensus Building (AMACB) uses competing large language models, each prompted to inhabit a specific stakeholder's worldview, to iteratively attack and refine a proposed framework until it survives all parties' strongest objections simultaneously.

**Key properties:**

1. **Ego-free adversarial testing.** An AI prompted to represent CCP interests will call a proposal "neocolonial" without diplomatic hedging. An AI prompted as an NSC red team will find the national security vulnerabilities without career risk.

2. **Rapid iteration.** A framework that would take months of back-channel diplomacy to evolve through objection-response cycles can be iterated in hours.

3. **Ideological triangulation through model selection.** Different AI models carry different institutional biases. Google Gemini (government/defense-adjacent), xAI Grok (libertarian/anti-establishment), and Anthropic Claude (safety-focused/analytical) produce genuinely different objection profiles.

4. **Human arbitration.** The human operator catches conflation errors the AIs miss, makes value judgments the AIs defer on, and applies political instinct no model can replicate.

5. **Transparent audit trail.** Every iteration, objection, correction, and pivot is preserved and published alongside the final framework.

### The Process

**Phase 1: Initial Framework** — The human proposes an initial structure. This will be wrong. The initial proposal's job is to give the AIs something concrete to attack.

**Phase 2: Adversarial Inhabitation** — Each AI model is prompted to inhabit a specific stakeholder position and critique the framework from that perspective with structurally honest objections.

**Phase 3: Iterative Refinement** — The human processes objections, decides which are valid, and revises. Invalid objections are discarded. Valid objections trigger structural changes.

**Phase 4: Stress Testing** — Blackout scenarios, bad actor scenarios, edge cases, and exploit hunting.

**Phase 5: Architecture Freeze** — When the framework survives all adversarial rounds without requiring structural changes, it is frozen.

### Case Study: Lattice Protocol v10.0

**Participants:** Google Gemini, xAI Grok, Anthropic Claude, CCP policy simulation, NSC red team simulation, human arbiter.

**Evolution:**
- V1 (4/10): "Leveraged Buyout" — rejected by CCP sim as neocolonial
- V3 (6.5/10): "Sovereign Infrastructure Protocol" — still too extractive
- V7 (7/10): "Restoration and Solvency Framework" — failed blackout stress test
- V10 (10/10): "Algorithmic Inevitability" — survived all stress tests

**Key human corrections (not caught by AIs):**
- 51% equity does not equal $660B (conflation)
- US total debt ~$39T, China holds ~$694B (scale error)
- "Pilot territory" is colonial; must be pluralistic
- 100% reversion post-debt = zero ROI = no investors
- Denominate in dollars, not credits or yuan
- $120B goes to builds, not wallets

**Key AI corrections (not caught by human):**
- CCP sim rejected early framing as neocolonial extraction
- Gemini identified 100% reversion kills ROI
- Grok persona collapse revealed Starlink vendor lock-in risk
- CCP sim demanded respect for PBOC oversight

### Applications Beyond Geopolitics

This methodology applies to any domain where competing interests must agree on shared frameworks: corporate mergers, trade agreements, regulatory design, labor negotiations, standards bodies, climate agreements.

### Requirements

- Multiple AI models from different organizations (minimum three)
- Specific persona prompting (not generic critique)
- Human arbitration willing to kill their own ideas
- Transparent logging of every iteration
- Willingness to be wrong at the start

### Limitations

- AIs can generate plausible-sounding but structurally empty objections
- Models may share training-data biases creating shared blind spots
- Produces architecturally sound frameworks, not politically viable ones
- Persona fidelity varies by model and topic
